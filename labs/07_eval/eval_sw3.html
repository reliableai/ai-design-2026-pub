"Eval" in Software 3.0 (AI Agents)

When there is no right answer — LLM-as-judge, rubric design, and building eval datasets

01 / NEW CHALLENGES

What's different in AI-powered systems

AI-powered systems generate open-ended outputs: summaries, conversations, decisions, code. Often there is no single "right answer" to compare against.

New challenges

  • No ground truth for many tasks — what's the "correct" summary of a meeting?
  • Human judgment is expensive and doesn't scale to thousands of test cases
  • LLM-as-judge as a scalable proxy — with its own failure modes (position bias, verbosity bias, self-preference)
  • Rubric design becomes a first-class engineering skill — vague rubrics produce unreliable judges
  • Eval datasets are hard to build — you need representative, diverse, adversarial examples
  • Evaluation is continuous — not a one-shot gate but an improvement loop
Key property

You often can't write down the expected output. Evaluation itself becomes an AI problem — you use LLMs to judge LLM outputs, and you need to evaluate your evaluator.

02 / LLM-AS-JUDGE

LLM-as-Judge

When human evaluation doesn't scale, we turn to LLMs as automated judges. The idea is simple: ask a (typically stronger) model to evaluate the output of another model against a rubric.

Why LLM-as-judge?

  • Scale: Evaluate thousands of examples in minutes, not weeks
  • Consistency: Same rubric applied uniformly (no annotator fatigue)
  • Cost: Orders of magnitude cheaper than human annotation at scale
  • Iteration speed: Fast feedback enables rapid development cycles

Known failure modes

  • Position bias: Judges prefer the first (or last) response when comparing two outputs
  • Verbosity bias: Longer responses are rated higher regardless of quality
  • Self-preference: Models prefer outputs that match their own style
  • Sycophancy: Judges may agree with confident-sounding but wrong outputs
  • Inconsistency: Same judge, same input, different scores across runs
Key insight

An LLM judge is itself a system that needs evaluation. You need to measure agreement with human judgments, track inter-run consistency, and watch for systematic biases.

03 / RUBRIC DESIGN

Rubric design as engineering

The quality of your LLM judge is bounded by the quality of your rubric. A vague rubric produces noisy, unreliable scores. A precise rubric produces consistent, actionable signal.

What makes a good rubric?

  • Specific criteria: "Is the summary accurate?" is vague. "Does the summary contain all key decisions from the meeting?" is actionable.
  • Clear scale anchors: Define what a 1, 3, and 5 look like with concrete examples
  • Independent dimensions: Score accuracy, completeness, and tone separately — not as one muddled number
  • Negative examples: Show the judge what failure looks like, not just success
Key insight

Rubric design is prompt engineering for evaluation. Invest as much care in your judge prompt as you do in your system prompt.

04 / EVAL DATASETS

Building evaluation datasets

Your eval is only as good as the data you evaluate on. A biased or unrepresentative eval set will systematically mislead you.

Dataset design principles

  • Representative: Cover the real distribution of inputs your system will see in production
  • Diverse: Include edge cases, adversarial inputs, and different user intents
  • Versioned: Track changes to your eval set — changing the test changes the score
  • Sized appropriately: Too small and your metrics have wide confidence intervals; too large and you waste resources
Key insight

Eval datasets are a product, not an afterthought. They need curation, versioning, and continuous improvement — just like the system they evaluate.
